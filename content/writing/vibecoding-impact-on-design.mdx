title: Vibecoding and the impact on design
new: true
---

I’ve started noticing a subtle shift - specifically within internal tools at Zoox - that feel like a leading indicator of broader change. What's changing is that ideas now arrive as working software. An engineer can vibe-code a functional tool over a weekend. A PM can translate a product requirements doc into an interactive prototype in an afternoon. I think the volume of working things is going to increase significantly over the next 9-12 months. I'm seeing it happen in internal tools, and I expect these are just the tip of the iceberg. 

If this trend expands (which most sources agree it will most definitely expand), it creates some new pressures on how we build operations tools:

Vibecoded tools are often built for a specific need, by a specific person, and then expand. When they mature into something the org depends on, they need product, design, and engineering investment. But by then inertia has already shaped what they are. We're going to need better ways to triage what deserves stable ownership and what should stay a side project.

In many ways this isn’t new. We’ve relied on hackathon projects for years. We build fleet support tools - that we depend on every day - on top of a weekend project. However, what feels new is the speed at which these tools can be built and metabolized by the org.

We used to filter ideas and projects before they became artifacts. Now we're evaluating artifacts that already exist and sometimes already have users. The inertia to ship something that already "works" is harder to redirect than the inertia to pursue an idea on paper.

At the end of the day, our operators don't have the option to stop using our tools. There's no churn or external forcing function to drive quality. UX debt already accumulates quietly. In an environment where more things are being built faster by more people, design has to work harder to hold a quality bar before it becomes a problem.

**What this means for designers at Zoox**

Every designer on the team already expected to ship to production. The goal isn't to replace engineering work. The hardest parts of software haven't changed. But designers who can generate trustworthy, compatible code can have a greater influence on quality, will have a higher batting average, and be more likely to deliver a better operator experience.

We also need to close the remaining gaps in our design system documentation. We're on our way to having the infrastructure that lets design and cross-functional partners prototype within our design system. This is even more important now that authorship is broadening.

**But I think we need to go even further**

If the trends hold, the designers who will thrive in this environment are those who are fluent enough in our codebase and ways of working that their prototypes are legible and adoptable by engineering. They're comfortable operating at multiple altitudes: doing first-principles discovery work on one project while shipping incremental improvements on another. They advocate with artifacts, not just user needs or taste.

Those who always treats Figma as the final output, who wait for clean briefs before engaging, and who see the expanding circle of makers as a threat to their role rather than an expansion of their leverage - will struggle.

We will also need to broaden our critique and discovery process. Right now there's a risk that working prototypes compress the upfront thinking that should happen before building. The prototype feels done, which creates pressure to mature it — before we've answered whether it should exist in its current form, whether it overlaps with adjacent tools, or whether it's solving the right problem at the right altitude.

We’ll also need to expand who participates in crit and design reviews. When a designer, PM, or anyone generates a working artifact, that artifact should go through the same process as any other work. It gets evaluated against our core operational metrics and quality bar. The fact that it already exists and works doesn't change the standard of quality.

We don't have this fully figured out. In design leadership conversations across the industry, governance in this environment is an open question. But here's what I believe is true for Zoox specifically:

The captive user dynamic makes governance more important here than at most software companies. Our operators use what we give them. The quality bar has to come from inside because it won't come from the market. The risk of "good enough" compounding across many vibe-coded experiments will be quiet.

The opportunity is that we're not yet at the scale where this is unmanageable. This year is the year to build the culture, the systems, and the shared standards that let a broader group of makers build things that hold. Not by restricting who can make, but by making quality the path of least resistance.
