---
title: Think Like a Tree
---

I've been thinking about trees lately. Robert Moor has a new book called *In Trees* that describes how trees grow, survive, get wounded, and endure. I kept reading sentences about trees and hearing product decisions I'd watched people get wrong, including myself. Two tensions in particular haven't left me alone.

Trees branch toward light. Not toward capacity. Not because it's Q3 and the trunk has bandwidth. They branch when they can see a gap, actual available light, and they extend toward it specifically. I've watched teams do the opposite more times than I can count. We have a new team, so we need a new initiative. We have engineering cycles opening up, so let's build the thing we've been talking about. The branch gets built because it can be built, not because there's evidence that this is where the growth is.

Pruning is the harder discipline. A tree prunes when a branch stops returning energy. It's getting shaded out. It's not reaching light. It falls. I've been in rooms where everybody knew a feature was dead. The engagement wasn't there. The team working on it was demoralized. The roadmap kept it alive because removing it meant disappointing a few customers or admitting failure. The branch had stopped getting light months ago. We were still feeding it.

Before you branch, ask where the light is actually coming from. What problems are users solving with workarounds because you haven't solved them yet? Branch toward that. And when you're looking at what to cut, ask whether the branch is still returning energy. If not, what are you waiting for?

I've seen two versions of the ascend versus root failure and they look completely different from the outside.

The first: a team with a genuinely great vision, ascending hard and fast. Big bets. Bold moves. But they haven't sat with their users in six months. They're running on conviction, which got them this far, but conviction without ongoing contact becomes ideology. They stop learning. They start explaining.

The second: a team that has been in discovery so long it feels like a lifestyle. They've talked to hundreds of customers. They understand the problem with real depth. But they won't commit. Every new conversation surfaces another nuance.

There's a principle in forestry worth sitting with: root depth scales with height. A tree that grows tall on shallow roots is the first thing down in a storm. The fallen trees after a big wind event almost always have undersized root systems relative to their canopy. They grew fast. They ascended impressively. They didn't build the foundation to hold the height. I've worked at a company that had this problem at a product level. The vision was extraordinary. But the understanding of actual users doing actual work was thin. The first serious market pressure, and the whole thing swayed.

Before you ascend, ask whether your roots can hold the height. And when you're tempted to stay in research forever, ask honestly: is this rooting, or is this hiding?

Here's the thing nobody tells you when you take a product leadership job: you will get this wrong. You will branch when you should have pruned. You will ascend before you were rooted. The products that endure aren't smooth and perfect. They're shaped by what they survived. The decisions that didn't pan out are in there, locked in the architecture somewhere, a detour in the trunk that can't be straightened out. You don't fix those by pretending they didn't happen. You grow from them.

What I've learned is that the teams that get this right aren't necessarily smarter. They're better at reading the signals that tell them which mode to be in. They look outward before they look inward. They ask where the light is before they ask what they have capacity to build. They check their roots before they commit to the height.

That's not a framework. It's an orientation. And like most useful things, it's simpler to describe than it is to hold onto when you're in the middle of a planning cycle and everyone is asking what we're going to build next.
